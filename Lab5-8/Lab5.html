Higgs Classification¶

In this project we are going to look at an inclusive search for the standard model Higgs boson in pp collisions at s = 13 TeV at the LHC using simulated data [A. Schuy]. The Higgs bosons are produced with large transverse momentum (pT) and decaying to a bottom quark-antiquark pair. The Higgs candidates could be reconstructed as large-radius jets using Calorimeters. Due to large QCD backgorund containmination, the direct 5-sigma observation of this Higgs channel is not accomplished yet[Phys. Rev. Lett. 120, 071802 (2018)]. We are going to use a set of training datasets to optimize event selections in order to enhance the discovery sensitivity. The optimal event selections will be applied to a selected pseudo-experiment data.

Lab 5a. Download the training datasets from one of the two pT-range folders. In each folder, there are 2 files, each containing 100k jets. The signal dataset is labeled as higgs and the background dataset is labeled as qcd.

I chose the high-pT samples.

The data has 14 variables which describe the properties of jets. eta and phi describes the orientation of the jet as specified in https://arxiv.org/pdf/1709.04533.pdf, Figure 3. pt and mass are the transverse momentum and mass of the jet in GeV. The other variables are given in https://arxiv.org/abs/1201.0008, Table 1. ee2 and ee3 are ratios of Energy Correlation Functions. d2 = ee3/ee2^3 is used to identify two body stuctures in jet. t1, t2, and t3 are the 1, 2, and 3-subjettiness. t21 = t2/t1 and t32 = t3/t2 are used to identify 2 and 3-body structures within jets, respectively. The smaller d2, t21 or t32 is, the more likely the jet is to have a 2 or 3-body substructure.

Below I plotted histograms of both signals (higgs) and the background (qcd) for each of the 14 variables to see if the variables provide discrimination power between the signal and the background.

def compare(param, ax, sigmas = 4, num_bins = 500):
    left1 = np.min([np.min(qcd[param]),np.min(higgs[param])])
    left2 = np.min([np.mean(qcd[param])-sigmas*np.std(qcd[param]), np.mean(higgs[param])-sigmas*np.std(higgs[param])])
    left = np.max([left1,left2])
    right1 = np.max([np.max(qcd[param]),np.max(higgs[param])])
    right2 = np.max([np.mean(qcd[param])+sigmas*np.std(qcd[param]), np.mean(higgs[param])+sigmas*np.std(higgs[param])])
    bins = np.linspace(np.max([left1,left2]),np.min([right1,right2]),num_bins)
    bins2 = np.linspace(np.max([left1,left2]),np.min([right1,right2]),num_bins)
    
    ax.hist(qcd[param], bins=bins2, alpha = 0.5, color= 'b',label = 'Background', ls='dashed')
    ax.hist(higgs[param], bins=bins, alpha = 0.5,color= 'r',label = 'Signal')
    ax.legend(prop={'size': 10})
    ax.set_ylabel("Counts")
    ax.set_xlabel(param)
    ax.set_title('Histograms of %s' % param)

As shown, the phi histograms for the background and the signal look the same. And the t32 histograms also look similar. The histograms between background and signal for the other parameters seem to have significant differences. Thus I think all parameters except phi and t32 provide some discrimination power between signal and background.

To see if there are correlations among the parameters, I calculated Pearson's r values for each possible pair of parameters within both the background and the signal dataset and printed the pairs of parameters with their r values' absolute values larger than 0.1. I ignored the parameters ee2, ee3, t1, t2, and t3 since I found that they are mostly corrolated to each other or d2, t21, and t32, which are derived from them.
